## 1. Concept Brainstorm

Your "Fern Gully transition" concept is strong and emotionally legible for an Earth Day crowd. Here are three variations on it and one alternative:

**A. "The Transition" (your original, refined)**
A vertical gradient scene: Wisconsin northwoods and Lake Michigan bluffs occupy the upper two-thirds, rendered in lush watercolor or painterly style. Gradually, the treeline thins, the soil turns gray, and a data center rises from the cleared land below. Factoid bubbles or cutout cards appear at key points along the transition, like trail markers. This is the most visually compelling and emotionally complete concept.

**B. "Same Land, Two Futures"**
A split or diptych format. Left panel: the Great Lakes landscape as it exists. Right panel: the same geography with a data center campus imposed on it, smoke rising, the lake diminished. Works well if you want stark contrast rather than gradual transition. Simpler to produce but less poetic.

**C. "The Invisible Pipeline"**
More abstract/diagrammatic. A person looking at their phone is the starting point. Lines flow from the phone upward into a network, which routes down to a data center that draws from a river or lake. This makes the personal connection explicit but sacrifices the emotional landscape imagery. Better as a supplemental panel than a hero visual.

**D. "What's Downstream"**
Metaphorical take: a Wisconsin river scene, idyllic above, but below the surface the riverbed shows phone icons, server racks, and data cables. Speaks to what's hidden beneath the surface of our daily habits. Requires more interpretive effort from the viewer but is more conceptually interesting.

**My recommendation:** Stick with Concept A. The gradual transition is emotionally correct for Earth Day, and it lets the landscape do the persuasion rather than asking the viewer to decode a metaphor.

## 2. How Ideogram Can Help

Ideogram is well-suited for exactly this kind of illustrative, painterly poster work. Here's how to use it strategically:

**Generate the hero image in sections.** Don't try to get the whole poster in one prompt. Instead:
- Prompt 1: The lush top half (northern Wisconsin forest, bluffs, lake, clear sky, painterly/watercolor style, no text)
- Prompt 2: The cleared-land bottom half (barren clearing, industrial data center, similar style to match the top)
- Stitch them together in Canva with a gradient or hand-drawn transition layer

**Style keywords that will serve you well:**
"gouache illustration," "editorial watercolor," "hand-painted," "lush Great Lakes forest," "Wisconsin bluffs," "environmental poster art," "Fern Gully aesthetic," "muted naturalistic palette" — these will push it away from generic AI imagery and toward something with the right emotional register.

**Use Ideogram for texture and accent elements too:**
- Individual tree/fern illustrations to use as design accents around factoid boxes
- A small decorative map outline of the Great Lakes region
- A stylized phone or server rack icon in the same painterly style for visual contrast

**Avoid asking Ideogram to include text directly.** Ideogram text rendering can be unreliable. Place all copy in Canva instead, which gives you full typographic control.

## 3. Factoid Suggestions

These are organized by theme, sourced from your site and the research above. I'll flag which ones are most locally resonant for a Madison Earth Day crowd.

---

**Factoid 1 — The Wisconsin Connection (highest local impact)**
A $15 billion data center campus is now under construction on 672 acres in Port Washington, WI, about 30 miles north of Milwaukee, as part of the Stargate AI program. It's part of OpenAI and Oracle's $500 billion national push to expand AI infrastructure. At peak capacity, it could draw up to 1.3 gigawatts of power — enough to power a large city.

A good poster-ready line: *"A $15B data center is being built 150 miles from here. It will need power equivalent to a major city."*

---

**Factoid 2 — The Water Problem**
Data centers nationally may withdraw as much as 150 billion gallons of water over the next five years, equivalent to the annual water use of 4.6 million households.

In 2023, indirect water use from the power that feeds data centers totaled 211 billion gallons nationally.

Poster-ready line: *"Data centers are thirsty. In 2023, U.S. data centers consumed water equivalent to millions of households — and demand is doubling."*

---

**Factoid 3 — The Great Lakes Are the Target**
Tech companies are eyeing the Great Lakes region specifically because of its cool climate and abundant freshwater. Water resource conflicts have already surfaced in southwestern Michigan, Minnesota, Wisconsin's Central Sands region, and Indiana.

Poster-ready line: *"Big Tech is coming to the Great Lakes for our water. Conflicts over overextraction are already underway in Wisconsin and neighboring states."*

---

**Factoid 4 — Your Scroll Has a Carbon Cost**
The tech industry now accounts for roughly 7% of global emissions, a number expected to rise with data center growth. The average user of one major short-video platform generates roughly 48 kg of CO2 per year just from scrolling. That's equivalent to driving a car over 100 miles.

Poster-ready line: *"Every hour of mindless scrolling has a carbon cost. Multiply that by billions of users every day."*

---

**Factoid 5 — The Energy Demand Surge**
U.S. data centers currently consume 4-5% of total electricity and are projected to reach 12% by 2030. The Great Lakes region alone is expected to see a 2-3% annual rise in energy demand over the next decade, driven largely by AI and data center growth.

Poster-ready line: *"AI and algorithm-driven apps could triple data centers' share of U.S. electricity by 2030. That power has to come from somewhere."*

---

**Optional Factoid 6 — The Jobs Promise vs. Reality**
Most data centers employ between 10 and 100 permanent workers, with construction providing the bulk of temporary jobs. The promise of economic development often outpaces what communities actually receive in return for the land, water, and power they give up.

This is a good "surprising" factoid that can prompt reflection without being alarmist.

---

**Suggested selection for the poster (5 final picks):**
I'd go with 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 — they build a logical chain from "your phone" to "our region's water." Factoid 6 is great for conversation at the booth but less suited to a poster because it requires more context to land.
